# Framework Quality Criteria

This document defines the standards for accepting new frameworks into the Strategic Frameworks library. All frameworks must meet these criteria to be added.

---

## Overview

The Strategic Frameworks library maintains high quality standards to ensure every framework:
- Is real and proven, not AI-generated or made-up
- Has credible origins and documented history
- Provides practical value to users
- Can be applied through facilitated workshops
- Adds unique value to the library

**Philosophy:** Quality over quantity. Better to have 40 excellent frameworks than 100 mediocre ones.

---

## Fatal Disqualifiers

These issues automatically disqualify a framework. If any are present, **DO NOT ADD** the framework.

### 🔴 Fatal Disqualifier 1: AI-Generated or Made-Up Framework

**What it looks like:**
- Framework has no clear creator
- Created by AI (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) on request
- No historical record or documentation
- Appears to exist only in AI outputs
- "Framework" is just list of generic questions

**Why it disqualifies:**
Fictional frameworks have no real-world validation. Library must contain only proven methodologies.

**How to detect:**
- Search for framework name - do results exist from before 2022?
- Can you find creator's name and credentials?
- Do any credible sources discuss it?
- Does it appear in books, papers, or professional publications?

**Examples of disqualifying phrases:**
- "I created this framework for you..."
- "This is a framework I designed..."
- "Here's a framework to help with..."
- No creator name or source

---

### 🔴 Fatal Disqualifier 2: No Credible Sources

**What it looks like:**
- Only random blogs mention it
- All sources are marketing material
- Can't find original publication
- No independent validation
- Sources contradict each other significantly

**Why it disqualifies:**
Without credible sources, framework's validity can't be verified. Users need resources to learn more.

**Minimum requirement:**
- At least 3 credible sources
- At least 1 substantial source (book, academic paper, major publication)
- Sources should be independent (not all from creator)

**Credible sources:**
✅ Published books (with ISBN)
✅ Academic papers (peer-reviewed)
✅ Major business publications (HBR, McKinsey Quarterly)
✅ Framework creator's official documentation
✅ Professional publications

**Not credible:**
❌ Anonymous blogs
❌ Marketing material only
❌ Personal websites with no credentials
❌ AI-generated content
❌ Unverifiable sources

---

### 🔴 Fatal Disqualifier 3: Duplicate of Existing Framework

**What it looks like:**
- Essentially same framework as one already in library
- Only difference is naming or branding
- Repackaged version with cosmetic changes
- No meaningful distinction

**Why it disqualifies:**
Duplicates confuse users and dilute library value. Each framework must offer unique value.

**How to check:**
- Review frameworks-index.md
- Compare methodology and structure
- Assess whether differences are meaningful
- Consider user perspective: "Would having both help or confuse?"

**When it's okay to add similar framework:**
- Meaningfully different methodology
- Different application context
- Complementary rather than competitive
- Fills specific gap

**Example:**
- ❌ Don't add: "Innovation Canvas" that's identical to Business Model Canvas
- ✅ Okay to add: Value Proposition Canvas (complements Business Model Canvas)

---

### 🔴 Fatal Disqualifier 4: Fundamentally Flawed Methodology

**What it looks like:**
- Methodology is incoherent or contradictory
- Based on false premises or pseudoscience
- Proven to be ineffective
- Widely discredited by experts
- Violates ethical principles

**Why it disqualifies:**
Framework would mislead users or produce bad outcomes. Library must provide reliable methodologies.

**Warning signs:**
- Expert consensus disagrees with framework
- Research shows it doesn't work
- Based on debunked theories
- Promotes unethical practices
- Logical contradictions in methodology

---

### 🔴 Fatal Disqualifier 5: Controversial or Unethical Framework

**What it looks like:**
- Promotes manipulation or deception
- Discriminatory or harmful
- Widely controversial for good reason
- Violates professional ethics
- Legal or regulatory concerns

**Why it disqualifies:**
Library must maintain ethical standards. No framework that could cause harm.

**Examples:**
- Manipulative sales techniques
- Discriminatory decision-making frameworks
- Frameworks promoting illegal activities

## Required Elements

All frameworks must have ALL of these elements to be added.

### ✅ Required 1: Real Creator with Credentials

**What's needed:**
- Creator's full name(s)
- Creator's credentials (relevant title, education, experience)
- Creator's area of expertise
- Reason creator is credible source

**Why it matters:**
Creator's credibility validates framework. Users trust frameworks from recognized experts.

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: "Michael Porter, Harvard Business School professor, creator of competitive strategy theory"
- ✅ Good: "Clayton Christensen, Harvard professor, expert in innovation and disruption"
- ❌ Bad: "Anonymous" or "Various sources"
- ❌ Bad: "AI" or "Community-created"

**How to verify:**
- Search creator's name
- Check LinkedIn, Wikipedia, academic profiles
- Verify credentials with institution
- Confirm expertise area matches framework

---

### ✅ Required 2: Clear Origin and Publication Date

**What's needed:**
- Year framework was created/published
- Original publication (book, paper, article)
- Context of creation (why was it created?)
- Historical background

**Why it matters:**
Origin story provides context and verifies framework is established, not new and untested.

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: "Published in 'Competitive Strategy' (1980) by Michael Porter"
- ✅ Good: "Introduced at Stanford d.school in 2005, codified in 'Change by Design' (2009)"
- ❌ Bad: "Recently developed" with no specific date
- ❌ Bad: No identifiable origin or publication

**How to verify:**
- Find original book or paper
- Check publication date
- Verify with publisher or journal
- Look for framework's history

---

### ✅ Required 3: Structured Methodology with Clear Steps

**What's needed:**
- Defined phases, steps, or components
- Clear sequence or process flow
- Specific activities for each phase
- Expected outputs from each phase
- Coherent overall structure

**Why it matters:**
Framework must be actionable. Users need clear steps to follow.

**Minimum standard:**
- At least 3 distinct phases/steps
- Clear description of what to do in each
- Logical flow or sequence
- Practical to apply

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: "5 phases - Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test, each with specific activities"
- ✅ Good: "4-step cycle - Observe, Orient, Decide, Act"
- ❌ Bad: "Think about the problem and solve it" (too vague)
- ❌ Bad: No clear structure, just principles

**How to verify:**
- Can you diagram the framework?
- Are phases clearly distinct?
- Could someone follow the steps?
- Is it more than just questions?

---

### ✅ Required 4: Real-World Examples (At Least 2)

**What's needed:**
- Minimum 2 specific, documented examples
- Preferably 3+ examples
- Named companies or people (not "a company")
- Specific situation, application, and outcome
- Verifiable sources for examples

**Why it matters:**
Examples prove framework works in practice. Without real-world validation, framework is just theory.

**Quality criteria for examples:**
- **Specific:** Names, dates, details
- **Complete:** Situation → Application → Outcome
- **Credible:** Documented in verifiable source
- **Relevant:** Demonstrates framework value

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: "Airbnb used Design Thinking to improve listing photos in 2009, leading to 2-3x booking increase (documented in 'The Airbnb Story')"
- ✅ Good: "Intel used OKRs in 1970s to align organization (documented by Andy Grove in 'High Output Management')"
- ❌ Bad: "Many companies use this framework successfully" (no specifics)
- ❌ Bad: "A tech startup applied this to improve their product" (no names)

**How to verify:**
- Search for case studies
- Find examples in books or articles
- Verify company/person names
- Check that story is documented somewhere

---

### ✅ Required 5: Credible Sources (At Least 3)

**What's needed:**
- Minimum 3 credible sources
- At least 1 substantial source (book, academic paper, major publication)
- Sources are independent (not all from creator)
- Sources are verifiable (can be found/purchased)

**Why it matters:**
Sources enable verification and provide users with learning resources.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Peer-reviewed academic papers (highest quality)
2. Books from established publishers
3. Major business publications (HBR, McKinsey, etc.)
4. Framework creator's official documentation
5. Professional blogs/articles (thoughtful analysis)

**Minimum acceptable:**
- 1 book OR 1 academic paper OR 1 major publication article
- Plus 2 additional credible sources

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: Original book + HBR article + Academic paper
- ✅ Good: Creator's published work + 2 independent analyses
- ❌ Bad: 3 random blog posts
- ❌ Bad: Only marketing material from creator's company

**How to verify:**
- Check book ISBN or paper DOI
- Verify publisher credibility
- Confirm author credentials
- Check publication date (not too old unless classic)

---

### ✅ Required 6: Practical Applicability

**What's needed:**
- Framework can be applied through facilitated workshop
- Clear questions for each phase
- Reasonable time commitment (30-90 minutes)
- Produces actionable outputs
- Can be documented in our format

**Why it matters:**
Framework must work in our use-framework system. Purely theoretical frameworks don't fit.

**Criteria:**
- Can be broken into workshop phases
- Has facilitation questions
- Produces insights or decisions
- Users can apply it themselves (after guidance)

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: Clear steps, can facilitate 60-min workshop, produces decision or strategy
- ❌ Bad: Requires 6-month consulting engagement
- ❌ Bad: Only creator knows how to apply it
- ❌ Bad: Purely theoretical with no practical steps

**How to verify:**
- Read implementation guides
- Check if workshops or courses exist
- Try to outline workshop structure
- Consider if you could facilitate it

---

### ✅ Required 7: Unique Value vs Existing Frameworks

**What's needed:**
- Framework offers something not already in library
- Difference is meaningful, not cosmetic
- Fills a gap or provides new perspective
- Would help users in situations existing frameworks don't

**Why it matters:**
Every framework should add value. Duplicates confuse users and dilute library quality.

**Assessment:**
- What does this framework offer that others don't?
- When would you use this instead of similar frameworks?
- Is the difference meaningful or just naming?
- Would having both frameworks help or confuse users?

**Examples:**
- ✅ Good: Porter's Five Forces (competitive) vs Blue Ocean Strategy (creating new markets) - complementary
- ✅ Good: Design Thinking (discovery) vs Lean Startup (validation) - different phases
- ❌ Bad: Two frameworks that do the exact same thing with different names

**How to verify:**
- Review frameworks-index.md
- Compare to similar frameworks
- Assess unique value proposition
- Consider user perspective

## Category Fit Assessment

Framework must fit into our taxonomy.

### Framework Categories

**Strategic Frameworks:**
- Focus on competitive positioning, market dynamics, long-term direction
- Examples: Porter's Five Forces, Blue Ocean Strategy, Wardley Mapping

**Decision Frameworks:**
- Focus on making choices, evaluating options, managing trade-offs
- Examples: OODA Loop, Pre-Mortem, Regret Minimization

**Innovation Frameworks:**
- Focus on creating new products/services, solving user needs, experimentation
- Examples: Design Thinking, Lean Startup, Jobs-to-be-Done

**Operational Frameworks:**
- Focus on execution, processes, metrics, optimization
- Examples: Theory of Constraints, OKR, Pareto Principle

**Mental Models:**
- Focus on thinking patterns, cognitive frameworks, perspective-taking
- Examples: First Principles, Second-Order Thinking, Inversion

**Assessment:**
- Does framework clearly fit one category?
- Could it work in multiple categories? (note secondary)
- Is it outside our taxonomy? (might not fit)

## Scoring Rubric

Use this to assess framework quality objectively.

### Fatal Disqualifiers (Any = Reject)
- [ ] AI-generated or made-up
- [ ] No credible sources
- [ ] Duplicate of existing framework
- [ ] Fundamentally flawed methodology
- [ ] Controversial or unethical

**If ANY fatal disqualifier:** **REJECT**

### Required Elements (All Must Pass)
- [ ] Real creator with credentials
- [ ] Clear origin and publication date
- [ ] Structured methodology (3+ phases)
- [ ] Real-world examples (2+ specific)
- [ ] Credible sources (3+, including 1 substantial)
- [ ] Practically applicable
- [ ] Unique value vs existing frameworks

**Score:** __/7 required elements

**Minimum to add:** 7/7 required elements

### Bonus Elements (Strengthen Framework)
- [ ] Multiple case studies (4+)
- [ ] Tools/templates available
- [ ] Academic validation
- [ ] Wide industry adoption
- [ ] Comprehensive learning resources

**Bonus score:** __/5 bonus elements

### Overall Assessment

**ACCEPT if:**
- No fatal disqualifiers (0/5)
- All required elements present (7/7)
- Fits taxonomy category

**REJECT if:**
- Any fatal disqualifier present
- Missing any required elements
- Doesn't fit taxonomy

**BORDERLINE if:**
- All required elements present
- But quality is marginal
- → Discuss with user, may accept with reservations

## Quality Control Questions

Before finalizing, ask:

**Creator & Origin:**
- Can I name the creator with credentials?
- Can I cite the original publication?
- Is the origin story credible and verifiable?

**Methodology:**
- Can I explain how this framework works?
- Are the steps clear and actionable?
- Could I facilitate a workshop using this?

**Validation:**
- Can I name 2-3 companies/people who used this?
- Can I describe specific examples with outcomes?
- Are these examples documented somewhere?

**Sources:**
- Can I cite 3+ credible sources?
- Do sources enable further learning?
- Are sources independent and verifiable?

**Value:**
- What unique value does this add?
- When would I recommend this over similar frameworks?
- Would users benefit from having this?

**If you can answer all positively:** Framework likely meets criteria

**If you hesitate on any:** May not meet quality standards

## Remember

**Library Reputation:**
Every framework we add reflects on the library's quality. One bad framework undermines trust in all frameworks.

**User Trust:**
Users trust us to provide proven, effective frameworks. We must maintain that trust through rigorous standards.

**Quality Over Quantity:**
40 excellent frameworks better than 100 mediocre ones. Be selective.

**When In Doubt:**
If unsure whether framework meets criteria, err on side of caution. Better to reject marginal framework than add questionable one.

**Standards Evolve:**
As library grows, standards may tighten. Framework that barely passes today might not pass later. Aim for clear passes.

## Examples

### ✅ ACCEPT: Design Thinking

- Creator: Tim Brown, David Kelley (IDEO) ✅
- Origin: 2008, "Change by Design" ✅
- Structure: 5 clear phases ✅
- Examples: Airbnb, Apple, IBM ✅
- Sources: Book, HBR articles, Stanford d.school ✅
- Applicable: Clear workshop structure ✅
- Unique: Human-centered innovation ✅

**Verdict:** Strong framework, clearly meets all criteria

### ❌ REJECT: "Strategic Alignment Matrix"

- Creator: Blog post by unknown author ❌
- Origin: Appears in 2023 blog only ❌
- Structure: Vague 4-box matrix
- Examples: "Many companies use this" ❌
- Sources: Only that blog post ❌
- Applicable: Too vague
- Unique: Similar to existing frameworks

**Verdict:** Fails multiple required elements, likely AI-generated

### 🟡 BORDERLINE: "Emergent Framework X"

- Creator: Academic researcher, limited credentials
- Origin: Recent paper (2022), limited validation
- Structure: Clear 6 phases ✅
- Examples: 1 detailed, 1 vague (needs 2+ clear)
- Sources: Academic paper + 1 article (marginal)
- Applicable: Yes ✅
- Unique: Somewhat unique ✅

**Verdict:** Borderline - discuss with user. May accept if user strongly advocates or reject as too new/unproven.
