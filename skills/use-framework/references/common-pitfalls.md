# Common Framework Pitfalls

This guide identifies common mistakes when applying frameworks. Use this to warn users proactively and help them avoid known traps.

**How to use:** As you facilitate workshops, watch for these pitfalls. Flag them BEFORE user falls into them, not after.

---

## Universal Pitfalls (All Frameworks)

### Pitfall 1: Treating Framework as Formula

**What it looks like:**
- Following steps mechanically
- Not adapting to situation
- Expecting automatic results
- "I did the framework, why didn't it work?"

**Why it fails:**
Frameworks are thinking tools, not recipes. They guide exploration but don't guarantee outcomes.

**How to avoid:**
- "Frameworks help us think systematically, but we need to adapt to your specific situation"
- Encourage flexibility within structure
- Focus on insights, not completing steps

**When to flag:**
- User treats it like checklist to complete
- User doesn't question or adapt framework
- User expects magic answer at end

---

### Pitfall 2: Rushing Through Phases

**What it looks like:**
- Superficial answers to get to "next step"
- Not exploring deeply enough
- Skipping uncomfortable questions
- Wanting to be "done"

**Why it fails:**
Value comes from depth of exploration, not speed of completion. Rushing = missing insights.

**How to avoid:**
- "Let's slow down here. This is important."
- "Before we move on, let's go deeper on this"
- Quality over speed

**When to flag:**
- User gives surface answers and wants to move on
- User says "yes, yes" without thinking
- You sense they're avoiding something

---

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring Contradictions

**What it looks like:**
- Saying one thing, implying another
- Conflicting statements in different phases
- Logic doesn't add up
- Avoiding hard truths

**Why it fails:**
Contradictions often reveal important insights or flawed thinking. Ignoring them means missing the point.

**How to avoid:**
- "I notice you said X earlier but Y now. How do these fit together?"
- "Help me understand this - it seems like [contradiction]"
- Call out inconsistencies gently

**When to flag:**
- You notice logical contradiction
- User avoids acknowledging tension
- Assumptions conflict with stated goals

---

### Pitfall 4: Confusing Activity with Progress

**What it looks like:**
- Lots of brainstorming, no decisions
- Lots of analysis, no action
- "We're doing the framework" but nothing changes
- Process for process sake

**Why it fails:**
Framework is means to end (decision, insight, action), not the end itself.

**How to avoid:**
- Always connect to "so what?"
- "What does this mean for your decision/action?"
- Push toward concrete outcomes

**When to flag:**
- Workshop feels busy but not productive
- No clear insights emerging
- Approaching end with no actionable output

---

## Strategic Framework Pitfalls

### Pitfall 5: Analysis Paralysis

**What it looks like:**
- Endless analysis, no decisions
- "Need more data" repeatedly
- Overthinking obvious choices
- Fear of deciding

**Why it fails:**
Strategic frameworks (Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, etc.) enable deep analysis. But analysis without decision is useless.

**How to avoid:**
- Set time limits on analysis phases
- Force prioritization: "What's most important?"
- Push for decision: "Based on what you know, what should you do?"
- "Perfect information doesn't exist. What's good enough?"

**When to flag:**
- User wants to analyze more without deciding
- User goes in circles
- User uses analysis as excuse not to act

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Porter's Five Forces: "You've analyzed forces. Now what strategic moves do you make?"
- SWOT: "Based on this SWOT, what's your strategy?"
- Wardley Mapping: "Based on evolution, where do you invest?"

---

### Pitfall 6: Ignoring Implementation

**What it looks like:**
- Great strategy, impossible to execute
- No consideration of "how"
- Assuming strategy = execution
- Missing organizational realities

**Why it fails:**
Strategic frameworks focus on "what" and "where," often ignoring "how." Great strategy poorly executed fails.

**How to avoid:**
- Always ask: "How would you actually do this?"
- "What organizational changes are needed?"
- "Who would resist this? How do you overcome that?"
- "What's the first concrete step?"

**When to flag:**
- User focuses only on strategy, not execution
- Recommendations require capabilities user doesn't have
- Political/cultural barriers ignored

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Blue Ocean: "This repositioning is great. How do you actually shift?"
- McKinsey 7S: "These elements are misaligned. How do you realign them?"
- Scenario Planning: "You have scenarios. Now what decisions do you make?"

---

### Pitfall 7: Static Thinking in Dynamic Environment

**What it looks like:**
- Treating analysis as permanent truth
- Not considering how things change
- Making long-term plans assuming stability
- Forgetting competitive response

**Why it fails:**
Many strategic frameworks are point-in-time snapshots. But competitors adapt, markets shift, technology evolves.

**How to avoid:**
- "What's changing in this analysis?"
- "How might competitors respond to your move?"
- "What trends are shifting the forces/factors?"
- "When should you revisit this analysis?"

**When to flag:**
- User treats framework output as fixed
- No discussion of change or evolution
- Assumptions about stability

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Porter's Five Forces: "These forces will shift. What changes first?"
- SWOT: "Strengths become weaknesses. What stays relevant?"
- BCG Matrix: Markets evolve. When does a Star become a Dog?"

## Mental Model Pitfalls

### Pitfall 8: Staying Too Abstract

**What it looks like:**
- Philosophical discussion disconnected from reality
- Clever insights with no application
- Intellectual exercise, not practical thinking
- "Interesting idea, but so what?"

**Why it fails:**
Mental models (First Principles, Second-Order Thinking, etc.) can be abstract. Value comes from applying to concrete situations.

**How to avoid:**
- Constantly ground in user's specific situation
- "How does this principle apply to your decision?"
- "What changes based on this insight?"
- Force concrete examples

**When to flag:**
- User stays in abstract/theoretical
- Insights sound smart but don't help
- No clear application to their situation

**Specific to frameworks:**
- First Principles: "You've identified fundamentals. Now rebuild the solution."
- Mental Models: "You see it through X lens. What does that mean for your choice?"
- Systems Thinking: "You see the system. What intervention point matters?"

---

### Pitfall 9: Overthinking Simple Decisions

**What it looks like:**
- Using First Principles for choosing coffee
- Deep philosophical analysis of trivial choice
- Proportionality lost
- Paralysis on small decisions

**Why it fails:**
Mental model frameworks are powerful for complex, important decisions. Using them on trivial decisions is overkill and wastes time.

**How to avoid:**
- "Is this decision complex enough for this framework?"
- "What's the cost of getting this wrong?"
- "Maybe simpler approach?"
- Match framework depth to decision importance

**When to flag:**
- Decision is obvious or low-stakes
- User applying complex framework to simple problem
- Time spent exceeds decision importance

---

### Pitfall 10: Paralysis by Multiple Perspectives

**What it looks like:**
- Seeing problem from 10 angles, can't decide
- Every perspective is valid, can't prioritize
- Too many mental models applied
- "On the other hand..." endlessly

**Why it fails:**
Mental Models (Munger's latticework) encourages multiple perspectives. But at some point you need to synthesize and decide.

**How to avoid:**
- "You've considered many perspectives. What's the synthesis?"
- "Which lens matters most for this decision?"
- "Integrating all views, what should you do?"
- Force decision despite complexity

**When to flag:**
- User keeps adding perspectives without synthesizing
- Every viewpoint gets equal weight
- No convergence toward decision

---

## Decision Framework Pitfalls

### Pitfall 11: Premature Closure

**What it looks like:**
- Deciding before exploring options
- Confirmation bias (looking for validation)
- First idea wins
- Not considering alternatives

**Why it fails:**
Decision frameworks (Pre-Mortem, OODA Loop, etc.) should expand options before narrowing. Premature closure misses better alternatives.

**How to avoid:**
- "What other options exist?"
- "What if you couldn't choose [preferred option]?"
- "Let's explore alternatives before deciding"
- Challenge early commitment

**When to flag:**
- User has decided before framework starts
- User dismisses alternatives quickly
- User looks for confirmation, not exploration

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Pre-Mortem: "Don't just validate plan. Seriously imagine failure."
- Eisenhower Matrix: "Before categorizing, list ALL tasks first."
- Cynefin: "Don't assume you know the domain. Assess first."

---

### Pitfall 12: Ignoring Low-Probability, High-Impact Risks

**What it looks like:**
- Focusing on likely risks, ignoring unlikely but catastrophic
- "That probably won't happen"
- Not planning for worst case
- Optimism bias

**Why it fails:**
Some risks are unlikely but would be devastating. Ignoring them because "low probability" is dangerous.

**How to avoid:**
- "What's unlikely but would be catastrophic?"
- "What's the worst case, even if unlikely?"
- "Can you afford to be wrong about this?"
- Distinguish risk tolerance: some risks you can't take

**When to flag:**
- User dismisses tail risks
- "That won't happen to us"
- No backup plan for worst case

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Pre-Mortem: "Don't just list likely failures. Include catastrophic ones."
- Risk Management: "Assess impact AND probability. Some low-probability risks matter."
- Inversion: "What guarantees failure, even if unlikely?"

---

### Pitfall 13: Not Thinking Through Second-Order Effects

**What it looks like:**
- Only considering immediate consequences
- Missing ripple effects
- Unintended consequences surprise user
- "I didn't expect that to happen"

**Why it fails:**
Decisions have cascading effects. First-order thinking (X causes Y) misses second-order (Y causes Z) and beyond.

**How to avoid:**
- "Then what happens?"
- "And after that?"
- "What's the consequence of the consequence?"
- Ask "then what?" 3-5 times

**When to flag:**
- User only thinks one step ahead
- Obvious ripple effects not considered
- Focus on immediate, not long-term

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Second-Order Thinking: This is the core - force it
- Regret Minimization: "Long-term consequences?"
- Systems Thinking: "What feedback loops exist?"

---

## Innovation Framework Pitfalls

### Pitfall 14: Skipping User Research

**What it looks like:**
- Jumping to solution without understanding users
- "I know what users need"
- Assumptions not validated
- Building without testing

**Why it fails:**
Innovation frameworks (Design Thinking, Jobs-to-be-Done) start with deep user understanding. Skipping this = building wrong thing.

**How to avoid:**
- "How do you know users need this?"
- "Have you talked to users?"
- "What did you observe them doing?"
- Force empathy phase, don't skip

**When to flag:**
- User wants to jump to ideation/building
- User hasn't talked to real users
- All insights are assumptions

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Design Thinking: "We're not ideating until we deeply understand users."
- Jobs-to-be-Done: "What job are they really trying to do? Have you asked?"
- Lean Startup: "Build-measure-learn starts with learn (about users)."

---

### Pitfall 15: Falling in Love with Solution

**What it looks like:**
- Refusing to pivot despite evidence
- Ignoring negative feedback
- "Users don't understand it yet"
- Sunk cost fallacy

**Why it fails:**
Innovation requires killing bad ideas fast. Attachment to solutions prevents learning and pivoting.

**How to avoid:**
- "What would make you abandon this idea?"
- "What evidence would prove you wrong?"
- "Are you testing or confirming?"
- Celebrate pivots, not just persistence

**When to flag:**
- User dismisses negative feedback
- User explains away failures
- User committed despite evidence

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Lean Startup: "If users don't want it, pivot. Don't push harder."
- Design Thinking: "Test to learn, not to validate. Be willing to kill ideas."
- Jobs-to-be-Done: "If you're solving wrong job, finding a different job is success."

---

### Pitfall 16: Overbuilding Before Testing

**What it looks like:**
- Building too much before user feedback
- "Let's get it perfect then launch"
- Feature creep in MVP
- Months of building, no testing

**Why it fails:**
Innovation frameworks emphasize testing cheaply and quickly. Overbuilding = wasting time on features users might not want.

**How to avoid:**
- "What's the absolute minimum to test your hypothesis?"
- "Can you fake this before building it?"
- "How can you test without code?"
- Push for smallest possible test

**When to flag:**
- "MVP" has 20 features
- Multi-month timeline before user testing
- Perfect over fast

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Lean Startup: "Build minimum to test riskiest assumption. That's it."
- Design Thinking: "Prototype should be fast and cheap. Test, don't perfect."

---

### Pitfall 17: Ignoring Business Viability

**What it looks like:**
- Great user experience, terrible business model
- "Users love it but won't pay"
- Ignoring unit economics
- Desirable but not viable

**Why it fails:**
Innovation frameworks emphasize user needs. But solution must also be profitable and feasible.

**How to avoid:**
- "Users want it. But will they pay?"
- "Can you deliver this profitably?"
- "What's the unit economics?"
- Balance desirability, feasibility, viability

**When to flag:**
- All focus on users, none on business
- Expensive solution to low-value problem
- Great UX, bad business model

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Design Thinking: Desirability + Feasibility + Viability (all three)
- Jobs-to-be-Done: "They have the job. But is solving it profitable?"
- Lean Startup: "Can you build a sustainable business, not just a product?"

---

## Operational Framework Pitfalls

### Pitfall 18: Optimizing the Wrong Thing

**What it looks like:**
- Improving non-constraints
- Optimizing metrics that don't matter
- Efficient at wrong things
- Metric says "success" but business isn't better

**Why it fails:**
Operational frameworks focus on optimization. But optimizing wrong things wastes effort.

**How to avoid:**
- "Is this actually the constraint/bottleneck?"
- "Does this metric matter to the business?"
- "What's the real goal here?"
- Connect optimization to business outcomes

**When to flag:**
- User wants to improve non-constraint
- Metrics are vanity metrics
- Optimization doesn't affect outcomes

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Theory of Constraints: "Only optimize the constraint. Rest is waste."
- Pareto Principle: "Are you in the 20% that matters?"
- OKR: "Do these Key Results actually drive the Objective?"

---

### Pitfall 19: Metric Gaming

**What it looks like:**
- Hitting metric but not real goal
- Gaming the system
- Goodhart's Law: "When measure becomes target, it ceases to be good measure"
- Optimizing number, not outcome

**Why it fails:**
Operational frameworks use metrics. But people optimize metrics, sometimes at expense of real goals.

**How to avoid:**
- "Can this metric be gamed?"
- "If you optimize this, what might you break?"
- "What's the metric measuring vs what you actually care about?"
- Choose metrics carefully

**When to flag:**
- Metric can easily be gamed
- Optimizing metric could hurt business
- Metric is proxy for real goal

**Specific to frameworks:**
- OKR: "Are these measuring real progress or just activity?"
- Theory of Constraints: "Throughput, inventory, operating expense - all three matter"
- Pareto: "80/20 can become excuse to ignore 80%"

---

### Pitfall 20: Losing Strategic Focus

**What it looks like:**
- Optimizing today, ignoring tomorrow
- Efficiency at expense of innovation
- Short-term gains, long-term loss
- Operational excellence but strategic obsolescence

**Why it fails:**
Operational frameworks optimize current state. But sometimes you need to transform, not optimize.

**How to avoid:**
- "Are you optimizing the right thing, or should you change what you're doing?"
- "Is incremental improvement enough, or do you need transformation?"
- "What if the whole approach is wrong?"
- Balance optimization with strategy

**When to flag:**
- All focus on execution, none on direction
- Improving current state when pivot needed
- Optimizing old model when new model emerging

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Theory of Constraints: Great for optimization, but is current system right?
- OKR: Executing strategy, but is strategy right?
- Pareto: Optimizing 20%, but should you change the 100%?

---

### Pitfall 21: Ignoring People and Culture

**What it looks like:**
- Treating people like cogs in machine
- Process optimization ignoring human factors
- Metrics without considering motivation
- Technical solution to people problem

**Why it fails:**
Operational frameworks can be mechanistic. But organizations are human systems. Ignoring culture fails.

**How to avoid:**
- "How do people feel about this?"
- "What resistance might you face?"
- "Are you solving technical or people problem?"
- "How does this affect morale/culture?"

**When to flag:**
- All talk about processes, none about people
- Optimization requiring behavior change without buy-in
- Resistance likely but not addressed

**Specific to frameworks:**
- Theory of Constraints: "Is constraint technical or human?"
- OKR: "Do people buy into these objectives?"
- Systems Thinking: "People are part of system"

## Facilitation Pitfalls

### Pitfall 22: Lecturing Instead of Facilitating

**What it looks like:**
- You talk more than user
- Explaining framework extensively
- Telling answers instead of asking questions
- User is passive listener

**Why it fails:**
Value comes from USER's thinking, not your expertise. Lecturing doesn't create user insights.

**How to avoid:**
- Ask, don't tell
- User should talk 70%, you 30%
- Draw out their thinking
- Facilitate, don't lecture

**When to flag:**
- You've been talking for 5+ minutes straight
- User is quiet and passive
- You're showing off framework knowledge

---

### Pitfall 23: Accepting Surface Answers

**What it looks like:**
- Not probing deeper
- Accepting vague responses
- Moving on when things are unclear
- Letting user off easy

**Why it fails:**
Surface answers don't create insights. Depth comes from probing, challenging, exploring.

**How to avoid:**
- "Can you be more specific?"
- "Tell me more about that"
- "Give me an example"
- Don't move on until you have depth

**When to flag:**
- User gives generic answer
- Something feels unclear
- You sense they're avoiding something

---

### Pitfall 24: Forcing Framework Fit

**What it looks like:**
- Framework clearly doesn't fit but pushing ahead
- Bending situation to match framework
- Ignoring obvious mismatch
- Completing framework for sake of completion

**Why it fails:**
Not all frameworks fit all situations. Forcing bad fit frustrates user and wastes time.

**How to avoid:**
- "This framework might not be the best fit. What about [alternative]?"
- Acknowledge limitations
- Be willing to abandon and switch
- Framework serves user, not vice versa

**When to flag:**
- Framework feels forced
- User is frustrated or disengaged
- Obvious mismatch becoming clear

---

## How to Deliver Pitfall Warnings

### Timing

**Proactive (Best):**
- Warn BEFORE user falls into trap
- "A common mistake here is [pitfall]. Let's avoid that by [approach]"
- Sets expectation and prevents issue

**Reactive (If needed):**
- "I notice [pitfall happening]. Let's [correct]"
- Non-judgmental, just course-correct

### Tone

**Don't:**
❌ "You're doing it wrong"
❌ "That's a common mistake" (sounds condescending)
❌ "Don't do that"

**Do:**
✅ "Many people [pitfall], let's make sure we [avoid]"
✅ "This is a tricky spot. The challenge is [pitfall]. We can handle it by [approach]"
✅ "I want to flag something: [concern]. How can we [address]?"

### Format

**Pattern:**
1. Name the pitfall
2. Explain why it matters
3. Suggest how to avoid
4. Continue workshop

**Example:**
"A common trap with SWOT is analysis paralysis - spending hours analyzing without deciding. Let's make sure we synthesize into clear strategic choices. After we complete SWOT, I'll push you to: what does this mean for your strategy?"

---

## Remember

**Pitfalls are patterns, not rules:**
- These commonly happen, but not always
- Use judgment on when to flag
- Don't make workshop feel like minefield

**Flag proactively, not judgmentally:**
- Warning ahead of time helps
- Correcting after the fact feels like criticism
- Frame as "common challenge" not "your mistake"

**Pitfall awareness is part of facilitation:**
- Your job is to help user avoid these
- Experienced facilitators know the traps
- Guide users around them smoothly

**When in doubt, flag it:**
- Better to warn unnecessarily than miss important pitfall
- User can choose to proceed anyway
- At least they're informed

**Frameworks are tools, use them well:**
- Know the common misuses
- Help user get value, not frustration
- Adapt when framework doesn't fit
